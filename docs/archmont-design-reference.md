# Archmont Design Reference

## Brand Position

Archmont Cleaners should feel like a premium residential cleaning service for busy households that want reliability without friction.

The product tone is:

- calm
- modern
- polished
- dependable
- discreetly premium

The brand should never sound like a marketplace experiment, a prototype, or internal operations software.

## Visual Direction

Use the supplied reference image as inspiration for atmosphere, not as a direct layout template.

Core visual cues:

- midnight-indigo and smoke-navy foundations
- cool blue and aqua glow accents
- frosted glass surfaces with soft transparency
- layered gradients and depth rather than flat fills
- rounded geometry and soft, elevated shadows
- subtle isometric or architectural illustration motifs in marketing moments

Do not apply heavy stylistic effects to dense data views if readability suffers.

## Layout Rules

### Marketing surfaces

- use large, editorial-style headlines
- pair strong copy with a premium visual panel or atmospheric graphic
- keep the primary CTA customer-first
- present cleaner access as secondary and quieter

### Auth and booking surfaces

- forms live inside lighter glass panels for readability
- keep the structure clear, calm, and conversion-focused
- group related fields into short sections with small helper text
- summary panels should feel elevated and trustworthy

### Dashboard surfaces

- preserve operational clarity first
- use brand color, shadow, and glow to elevate the experience
- next-action areas should stand out without becoming noisy
- status, timing, address, and service details must remain easy to scan

## Typography

- display headings: elegant, tightly tracked, high contrast
- body copy: clean sans-serif with excellent legibility
- dashboard and form typography: slightly more compact and practical

Preferred feel:

- display: refined and slightly architectural
- body: modern and neutral

Avoid:

- default-looking UI typography
- playful consumer startup copy
- overly technical labels

## Component Rules

### Header

- translucent dark-glass bar
- strong brand mark on the left
- customer actions prioritized in the nav
- secondary provider access visually quieter

### Hero

- one strong value proposition
- one primary CTA
- one or two support CTAs only
- atmospheric art panel or visual card cluster on the side

### Cards and panels

- rounded corners
- layered shadows
- light inner border or highlight
- dark-glass or luminous-surface treatment depending on context

### Forms

- high-contrast labels
- generous spacing
- restrained glow on focus
- helper copy in muted tone
- avoid overly glossy inputs

### Status badges

- soft capsule shapes
- semantic tinting by state
- readable even in dense dashboard layouts

### Tables

- cleaner, quieter styling than marketing panels
- clear row separation
- readable contrast
- use brand accents sparingly

### CTAs

- primary actions in luminous blue
- secondary actions in frosted neutral styles
- destructive actions visually obvious but not loud

## Copy Rules

- speak like a production service business
- emphasize confidence, convenience, quality, and reliability
- prefer plain language over technical phrasing
- remove any copy that references demos, prototypes, fallback logic, internal state, or seed data

Examples of preferred tone:

- "Book a home cleaning in minutes."
- "Your arrival window is confirmed."
- "A trusted professional is assigned to your visit."
- "Everything for your visit is organized in one place."

Avoid copy like:

- "working proof of concept"
- "customer flow"
- "create cleaner account" in public hero space
- "local demo payment fallback"
- "minimal admin view"

## Role Guidance

The public experience is customer-first.

- marketing, hero, and public navigation prioritize booking and returning customers
- cleaner access remains available, but should be labeled as provider or team access and visually de-emphasized
- authenticated customer and cleaner experiences should still feel part of the same brand system

## Product Presentation Defaults

- customers book with confidence
- timing is framed as a scheduled arrival window
- support language is human and reassuring
- dashboards should feel like service hubs, not internal tooling
